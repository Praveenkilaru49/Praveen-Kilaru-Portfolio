Thermal performance of organic phase change material for cooling of buildings

Overview

This project investigates the thermal performance of organic Phase Change Material (PCM), specifically Lauric Acid, in enhancing passive cooling for building envelopes. The aim was to evaluate the ability of PCM to reduce peak indoor temperatures and regulate heat flow through walls using both experimental and simulation methods.

Project Description

The primary focus of this study is the assessment of Lauric Acid as a PCM embedded in walls to improve thermal comfort and reduce cooling energy demands. An experimental test chamber was developed with two identical cubical rooms—one integrated with PCM and the other without—to compare thermal behavior under simulated solar radiation.

Real-time temperature readings from thermocouples placed at multiple points within both rooms revealed a significant reduction in peak temperatures in the PCM room. To further analyze thermal behavior, the experimental design was modeled in CATIA V5 and simulated in ANSYS Workbench to visualize temperature gradients and validate experimental results.

The simulations helped to understand the heat absorption and release cycle of Lauric Acid during phase transitions. It was observed that during daytime, PCM absorbed heat by melting and maintained lower room temperature. At night, it released heat by solidifying, keeping the room warmer compared to non-PCM walls. This approach demonstrates the practical application of PCMs in energy-efficient building design.

Tools Used

  • CATIA V5 for modeling
  • ANSYS Workbench for thermal simulation
  • Thermocouples and Arduino for temperature data acquisition
  • PCM: Lauric Acid (Melting Point ~ 43°C)

Skills Achieved

  • Thermal analysis and simulation
  • Experimental test bed development
  • CAD modeling and meshing
  • Data acquisition and interpretation

Conclusion

The inclusion of Lauric Acid PCM in wall assemblies effectively reduced temperature fluctuations, providing a more stable indoor climate. The results proved that organic PCMs can enhance energy efficiency and thermal comfort in buildings, particularly in regions with high diurnal temperature variations.
